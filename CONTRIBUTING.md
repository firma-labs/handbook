# Contributing to the Firma Labs Handbook

The handbook is a living document. It's meant to be improved over time by the people who live inside it. Contributions are welcome from the whole team — and from outside contributors who spot things we've missed.

## Who edits what

**Handbook maintainers (commit access on main):**

- DJ — Partner, Engineering
- Nic — Partner, Vision Operations
- Andi — Partner, Product

These three review pull requests and merge changes to the main branch.

**Everyone on the team:**

You can propose changes in one of three ways:

1. **Edit in GitBook** — if you have editor access, GitBook's web editor will commit changes back to GitHub automatically. Good for small copy edits.
2. **Open a pull request** — for anything bigger, fork the repo, make your changes, open a PR, and ping one of the maintainers.
3. **Open an issue** — if you spot something that should change but you don't want to propose the fix yourself, open an issue describing what and why.

**People Ops (Vanessa and team):**

The Compensation, Getting Started, and Policies chapters are maintained primarily by People Operations. Changes to those chapters flow through Vanessa, who either commits directly or routes them through one of the maintainers.

## Style notes

- **Plain language.** The handbook is written to be read, not to perform. Avoid jargon, avoid corporate-speak, avoid unnecessary hedging.
- **Short sentences.** The voice is direct. If a sentence runs more than 25 words, consider breaking it.
- **Second person when talking to the reader.** "You" — not "the employee."
- **No acronyms without expansion on first use.** USDC, PR, SEZ all get a first-use expansion.
- **Markdown, not HTML.** Stick to standard markdown. GitBook's theme handles the visual polish.

## What to avoid

- Don't name specific customers or partners without clearing it first.
- Don't name team members in public chapters unless they've agreed to be named.
- Don't describe unreleased products or ongoing negotiations.
- Don't make commitments on behalf of the company that would need legal review.

If you're unsure whether something is okay to publish, open an issue rather than committing directly. It's easier to have the conversation before a change lands than to revert afterward.

## How long does it take?

Small edits (typos, clarifications): reviewed and merged within a day or two.

Bigger changes (new sections, restructuring): discussed in an issue first, then implemented in a PR. A week or two depending on scope.

Major changes (new chapters, rewrites): require a conversation with the maintainers before drafting. Open an issue to start.
